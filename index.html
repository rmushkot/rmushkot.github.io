SAP Sponsorship

Jan 2020 - June 2020

Undergraduate Reseacher

For six months I worked on SAP's HANA database to improve the performance of a key function as a part of a sponsorship through UCSC. HANA servers over 32,000 enterprise customers all over the world with a purpose of providing a high performance, data-driven, and real-time decision-making database. HANA is constructed into two partions, one optimized for read operations and the other optimized for write operations. Due to the structure of HANA, a crucial operation called Delta Merge is used to merge data from the write partition into the read partition. Delta Merge handles millions of column entries every time it runs so speed and efficency is essential. You can find more information on the Delta Merge here.

Working with both SAP and Intel, my team and I aimed to increase the data throughput of the Delta Merge operation. Originally SAP's implementation was able to achieve a 5-10 GB/s throughput. Our plan to improve upon this was to use an Intel Field Programmable Gate Array (FPGA) in collaboration with OpenCL. An FPGA was chosen due to its parallel computing nature while OpenCL provides a convenient framework to work on FPGAs as well as assisting in optimizing code. By utilizing pipeline parallelism and vectorization techniques we were able to achieve a 200% increase in data throughput, improving it to 15-20 GB/s.

Storage Systems Research Center

Aug 2018 - June 2020



Undergraduate Reseacher

For nearly 2 years I worked for Storage Systems Research Center which Is a part of the Jack Baskin School of Engineering at the University of California, Santa Cruz. I worked under Distinguished Professor Darrell Long as part of the IMSI Catcher Dector team also called "Stingray". On Stingray we worked to detect and geolocate rogue cell phone towers, which are cell phone surveillance devices that mimic cell phone towers in order to trick mobile phones into connecting to it. This allows the owner of the surveillance device to collect any data that is sent to and from the mobile phone that is connected. Our team set out to create an application that would be able to detect and locate one of these devices, as they are often used to illegally spy on citizens.

On this project I was responsible for developing a database to store location and radio frequency data gathered from multiple Raspberry Pi clients in order to perform multilaterion calculations. This data was obtained through C code that I wrote for serial communication with GPS and radio modules. In addition, I assited in devising a communcation protocol for sending this data from the Raspberry Pis to our remote server. I also installed real-time operating systems on these clients to ensure they synchronously ran a radio sampling algorithm, achieving a 2% timing error.
